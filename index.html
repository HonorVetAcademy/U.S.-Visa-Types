The Foundations

Before H-1B, L-1, and every other acronym — six words decide whether a recruiter reads a candidate's status correctly. Get comfortable with these first.

Passport — The National ID Book

Think of it likeA passport is a person's national ID book — it belongs to their home country and proves who they are, wherever they travel.
In Plain Terms

A passport is an official travel document issued by a person's home government. It proves identity and nationality. An Indian citizen carries an Indian passport regardless of which country they visit or work in. A passport by itself does not grant the right to enter or work in the United States — that's what a visa is for.

Visa — The Ticket and Permission Slip

Think of it likeIf the passport is the ID book, a visa is the ticket and permission slip stuck inside it — issued by the country you want to visit, saying "yes, you may come to our border and request entry."
In Plain Terms

A visa is an entry-clearance stamp or sticker placed in a passport by a US Embassy or Consulate. It grants permission to travel to and seek entry into the United States — it is not, by itself, a guarantee of admission, and it is not automatically a work permit.

Common Error

"Has a visa" ≠ "Can work"

A visa only gets someone to the US border. Whether they can actually work once inside depends on their status and, often, a separate document called an EAD. That distinction is the backbone of this entire reference.

Status — What They're Allowed to Do Once Inside

Think of it likeIf the visa is the ticket that got someone through the door, status is what they're allowed to do after they're inside — and it can change even if the visa stamp itself doesn't.
In Plain Terms

Status is the legal category a person holds while physically in the United States. Someone can enter on an H-1B visa, and later their status changes — say, to H-4, or to a pending Green Card applicant — without a new visa stamp, because they haven't left and re-entered. This is why recruiters ask about current status, never just "what visa did you enter on."

Form I-94 — The Official Arrival Record

Think of it likeThe I-94 is the receipt US Customs hands over at the border — the official record of when someone arrived, in what status, and how long they may stay.
In Plain Terms

Form I-94 is the Arrival/Departure Record. It's often the real source of truth about current status — more current than the visa stamp, since status can change after entry. Candidates can check their own I-94 at the official CBP website using their passport details.

EAD — The Separate Work Permit Card

Think of it likeAn EAD is a separate work permit card — a physical, wallet-sized ID that specifically says "this person may legally work in the US," issued independently of the visa itself.
In Plain Terms

EAD stands for Employment Authorization Document — officially Form I-766, issued by USCIS. It is the actual proof a person can legally work, separate from whatever visa or status they hold. Not everyone needs one (an H-1B worker's visa itself authorizes work with their sponsor), but many categories — H-4 spouses, some L-2 spouses, OPT students, Green Card applicants — specifically need this card.

Say This Correctly

An EAD Is Not a Visa Type — It's a Document

"H-1B," "L-1," "F-1" are visa categories. "EAD" is not in that list — it's a physical card (Form I-766) that USCIS issues to prove work authorization, and it can sit on top of several different underlying statuses. There is no such thing as "an EAD visa." A candidate is never simply "on an EAD" — they hold a specific status (H-4, L-2, F-1/OPT, pending Green Card, etc.) and, depending on that status, may also hold an EAD card proving they can work. Always ask for the status first, then ask whether an EAD applies to it.

The Core Idea

Entry ≠ Employment

A visa gets someone into the country. Work authorization — through the visa itself, or through a separate EAD — is what allows them to legally work. These are not always the same thing, and confusing them is the single most common recruiter error. Every section below builds on this one distinction.

Social Security Number — The Payroll ID

Think of it likeAn SSN is a person's permanent employee ID number for the entire US system — used for payroll, taxes, and work history, issued once and never changed.
In Plain Terms

A nine-digit number issued by the Social Security Administration to citizens, permanent residents, and temporary workers authorized to work. Required for payroll and tax reporting — a candidate cannot be legally paid on US payroll without one. Confirm early whether a candidate already has an SSN or needs to apply after their status is approved.

Form I-9 — The Final Checkpoint

Think of it likeThe I-9 is the final checkpoint every US employer runs — confirming "we checked this person's documents and they are legally allowed to work here."
In Plain Terms

Every US employer must complete Form I-9 for every hire. The employee shows either one List A document (like an unexpired EAD or a US passport — proves identity and work authorization together) or one List B + one List C document (like a driver's license plus a Social Security card).

💡
Recruiter TipAsk a candidate what documents they can present for Form I-9 early in the screening call — the answer directly predicts whether onboarding goes smoothly or stalls at the client.
Section 02
SPONSORED WORK
Work Visas

The visas tied directly to a job — where the visa and the work authorization travel together.

H-1B
The most common visa recruiters encounter. A specialty occupation visa requiring a US employer sponsor and a role needing specialized knowledge (typically a bachelor's degree or higher). Initial approval covers up to 3 years, extendable to 6 years total, and further extendable under AC21 if a Green Card process is already underway. Employer files Form I-129 for petition, extension, or amendment.
H-1B1
A close cousin of H-1B reserved specifically for citizens of Chile and Singapore under Free Trade Agreement provisions. Also filed on Form I-129.
L-1A
Intra-company transfer visa for executives and managers moving from an overseas office to a US branch, subsidiary, or affiliate. Employer files Form I-129.
L-1B
Same intra-company transfer concept, for employees with specialized knowledge rather than management roles. Also filed on Form I-129.
TN
USMCA (formerly NAFTA) professional worker visa for Canadian (TN-1) and Mexican (TN-2) citizens in specific listed professions. Renewed indefinitely as long as the job qualifies. Extended in the US via Form I-129; Canadians can also renew at the border with no petition.
E-3
Reserved for Australian citizens in a specialty occupation. Functions like a simplified H-1B with its own, rarely-reached annual cap. US extensions filed on Form I-129.
B-1/B-2
Not work visas at all. B-1 is for business visitors; B-2 is tourism or medical treatment. Flag immediately if a candidate's only status is B-1/B-2.
2026 Update

The H-1B Lottery Changed

H-1B is subject to an annual lottery cap for new registrations. USCIS introduced a wage-weighted selection system effective February 27, 2026, replacing the old random lottery. Sponsorship for a brand-new H-1B is less predictable than in past cycles — always confirm current cap-subject status with the candidate rather than assuming based on past years.

Who Files What

I-129 Belongs to the Employer, Not the Candidate

Form I-129, Petition for a Nonimmigrant Worker, is filed by the employer — never the candidate directly — for H-1B, H-1B1, L-1A, L-1B, TN, and E-3 petitions, extensions, and amendments. If a candidate says "I'm filing my own extension," they likely mean a dependent filing (see the Family & Dependents section), not their own principal work visa.

💡
Recruiter TipWhen a requirement lists "H-1B acceptable," don't stop there — confirm the candidate's H-1B extension history. A candidate near their 6-year cap with no pending I-140 is a real placement risk on anything beyond a short contract.
💡

Think of it likeTwo families with a similar-looking guest pass — one family's pass lets the spouse work immediately, no extra paperwork; the other family's spouse needs to apply for a separate work badge first. They look similar on the surface, but the rules are completely different.

H-4 — Dependent of H-1B / H-1B1

In Plain Terms

H-4 is the dependent status for spouses and children of H-1B and H-1B1 holders. H-4 status alone does not authorize work. A subset of H-4 spouses may separately qualify for an H4-EAD — but this requires its own application and its own physical card.

1
Not automaticEligibility requires the H-1B spouse to have an approved I-140, or an H-1B extension beyond the 6-year cap under AC21 (PERM/I-140 pending 365+ days). The H-4 spouse files Form I-539 to extend or obtain H-4 status, and Form I-765 to apply for the EAD itself — often submitted together.
2
Physical card required — no exceptionsThe candidate must hold an unexpired EAD (Form I-766), category code C(26), before starting employment.
3
No more automatic bridge extensionRenewals filed on or after October 30, 2025 get no 540-day bridge — if the card expires before renewal is approved, the candidate cannot legally work.
4
Always check the card itselfDon't take a candidate's word for "I have an EAD" — ask to see it and confirm the dates are current.

L-2 — Dependent of L-1A / L-1B

In Plain Terms

L-2 is the dependent status for spouses and children of L-1A and L-1B holders. This is where L-2 and H-4 diverge sharply — and it's the update most recruiters miss.

Rule Change — Since Jan 30, 2022

L-2 Spouses Get Automatic Work Authorization

L-2 spouses receive work authorization incident to status — no separate EAD card required. USCIS and CBP issue an I-94 with the class-of-admission code "L-2S" for the spouse. An unexpired I-94 showing L-2S is itself acceptable proof of work authorization.

1
Spouse only, never childrenA child on plain "L-2" (no S) has no work authorization at all, automatic or otherwise.
2
Ask for the I-94, not just the visa stampProof is the I-94 showing "L-2S" — candidates can check this at the official CBP I-94 website. The L-2 spouse still files Form I-539 to extend or obtain L-2 status itself, even though no separate EAD application is needed for work authorization.
3
Legacy physical EAD still valid if issuedSpouses who entered before the 2022 change, or whose I-94 wasn't correctly annotated, may still hold a physical L2-EAD. Either document works.

Common Error

Treating H-4 and L-2 as Interchangeable

Both are "dependent visas that might have an EAD," but the rules, proof documents, and risk are completely different. When a candidate says "I'm on a dependent EAD," always ask which principal visa it's tied to before assuming what proof to request.

💡
Recruiter TipIf a candidate says "I don't need an EAD, I have automatic authorization," your very next question should be: "Is your spouse on an H-1B or an L-1?" That single answer tells you if the claim is even true.
Section 04
PERMANENT RESIDENCE
The Green Card Track

Two terms that sound similar but mean very different things — and confusing them on a submission is a common, avoidable error.

Green Card — Permanent Residence

Think of it likeA Green Card is like earning permanent residency status — the person can live and work in the US almost exactly like a citizen, for any employer, indefinitely.
In Plain Terms

Lawful Permanent Residence — the Green Card (GC) — allows a foreign national to live and work permanently in the United States, with almost no restriction on employer or role. An employment-based case starts with Form I-140, Immigrant Petition for Alien Worker, filed by the employer; a family-based case uses Form I-130 instead, filed by the relative.

How Green Cards Happen Through Employment

EB-1
Priority Workers — extraordinary ability, outstanding professors/researchers, multinational managers/executives
EB-2
Advanced-degree professionals, or exceptional ability substantially benefiting the US
EB-3
Bachelor's-degree professionals, skilled workers (2+ yrs experience), other workers
EB-4
Special Immigrants — religious workers and other special categories
EB-5
Investors — $500,000–$1,000,000 in a qualifying commercial enterprise

GC-EAD — The Interim Work Permit

Think of it likeIf the Green Card process is a long flight with a layover, GC-EAD is the temporary boarding pass for the layover — it lets someone work while they wait for the connecting flight to be ready.
In Plain Terms

The Green Card process — especially employment-based — can take a year to well over a decade depending on category and country of birth. Once Form I-485 (Application to Register Permanent Residence or Adjust Status) is filed and pending, the applicant files Form I-765 to request an EAD to work legally while the actual Green Card is still processing — typically paired with Form I-131 for Advance Parole (travel authorization) as a combo card.

1
Not employer-specificUnlike H-1B (tied to sponsor) or CPT (tied to school), a GC-EAD holder can work for any employer without new sponsorship.
2
Code C09GC-EAD typically carries category code C09. Cards are usually valid 1–2 years and must be renewed while the case is pending.
3
Never record GC-EAD as "GC"Some clients accept GC-EAD candidates; others specifically require the actual Green Card. Always clarify which the requirement calls for.
4
Ask where they are in the process"I-485 pending" plus a valid EAD confirms GC-EAD status. "I-140 approved" but no I-485 filed yet is an earlier stage.

Recruiter TipWhen a candidate says "I have my Green Card" but mentions their I-485 is still pending, they almost certainly mean GC-EAD, not an approved Green Card — clarify before you log their status in JobDiva or submit them against a requirement that specifically needs a full GC.
Section 05
ACADEMIC PATHWAY
Student Status — F-1, CPT & OPT

The most frequently misunderstood work-authorization pathway in IT staffing. Let's untangle it properly.

F-1 — The Base Student Visa

Think of it likeF-1 is a student membership card — it lets someone study at a US school, but on its own it barely lets them work at all.
In Plain Terms

F-1 is the non-immigrant visa for people studying at a US university, college, or other SEVP-approved institution, evidenced by Form I-20 (Certificate of Eligibility for Nonimmigrant Student Status), issued by the school. On its own, F-1 permits only limited on-campus work. Real employment eligibility comes through CPT or OPT — two very different mechanisms that get confused constantly.

CPT — Curricular Practical Training

Think of it likeCPT is an official school-sponsored internship — it only exists because it's part of the curriculum, and only with employers the school has approved.
In Plain Terms

Employment that's an integral part of the academic curriculum — internship, cooperative education, or required practicum. Must happen before degree completion, requires a job offer at the time of application, and is authorized term by term.

OPT — Optional Practical Training

Think of it likeOPT is more like a personal work permit tied to the student's degree field — it doesn't need a specific employer's approval up front, and it comes with an actual EAD card.
In Plain Terms

Work authorization related to the student's major, resulting in an actual EAD card from USCIS. Filed on Form I-765 — this is the same application form used for H4-EAD and GC-EAD, just under a different eligibility category. Can be used before or after graduation and doesn't require a job offer to apply. Standard OPT allows up to 12 months; STEM graduates can extend further.

CPT vs. OPT, Side by Side

FactorCPTOPT
TimingMust complete before graduationBefore or after graduation

Good to Know

STEM OPT Extension

F-1 students with a qualifying STEM degree can apply for a 24-month extension of post-completion OPT, for a total of 36 months. This replaced the earlier 17-month extension. The employer must be enrolled in E-Verify for the student to qualify. Before applying, the student and employer jointly complete Form I-983 (Training Plan for STEM OPT Students) with the school; the student then files Form I-765 with a new STEM-endorsed I-20.

Common Error

Treating OPT and CPT as Synonyms

Both come from an F-1 visa, but they have different proof documents, different employer restrictions, and different durations. A requirement that says "OPT candidates acceptable" does not automatically mean a CPT candidate qualifies.

💡
Recruiter TipAsk CPT candidates directly whether the employer on the job order is already on their school's approved SEVIS list — a great technical fit is not placeable if that box isn't checked.
💡
Recruiter TipFor OPT candidates nearing their 12-month mark, ask about STEM extension eligibility up front — it directly affects how long they can realistically stay on a project.
Section 06
DECODE THE CARD
EAD Category Codes

Every EAD card has a category code printed on its face. Learning to recognize the handful recruiters see most often is the fastest way to confirm what you're actually looking at.

Where to Find It

Look Near the Photo

On a physical EAD (Form I-766), the category code appears in a labeled field near the photo — like "C09" or "C33." This code refers to a specific subsection of 8 CFR 274a.12. Always ask the candidate to confirm this code directly rather than assuming based on what they call their own status.

Codes Recruiters See Most Often

Adjustment-of-status applicant — the standard GC-EAD code
C03B

A18
L-2 spouse of an L-1 intracompany transferee — legacy code, largely superseded by the L-2S I-94 rule
C(26)

Forms at a Glance

Every status change, extension, or work-authorization request runs through one of a handful of USCIS forms. Knowing which form belongs to which situation — and who actually files it — helps you set realistic timeline expectations with candidates and clients.

Timing Reality Check

Extensions Take Months, Not Days

Form I-539 processing commonly runs 6–14 months without premium processing; premium processing is now available for certain H-4, L-2, E, and O dependent categories at roughly 30 business days. If a dependent's status or EAD is expiring soon and no extension has been filed yet, that's a real placement risk — not a paperwork formality.

💡
Recruiter TipWhen a candidate mentions "my extension is in process," ask which form was filed and when — "I-129 filed by my employer last month" and "I-539 filed by me three weeks ago" tell you very different things about how much runway is actually left.
Section 08
FIELD PLAYBOOK
The Screening Playbook

Every fact in this reference exists to support one skill: asking the right questions during screening. This is the working checklist for every call.

Ask Every Candidate

1
What is your current visa status?Get the exact term — "H-1B," "H-4 with EAD," "GC-EAD," "OPT" — not a vague "I'm authorized to work."
2
Do you have a physical EAD card, or is your authorization tied to your visa/I-94 directly?This single question separates candidates like L-2 (I-94-based) from H-4 and OPT (EAD-card-based).
3
What is the category code on your EAD, if you have one?Cross-reference against the Reference Codes section. If they don't know it, ask them to check the card.
4
What is the expiration date on your current work authorization?Critical for any placement with real duration.
5
If a renewal is pending, when was it filed?For H4-EAD specifically — determines whether the old 540-day bridge extension applies.
6
Is your work authorization tied to a specific employer, or portable?H-1B, L-1, CPT are employer/school-tied. GC-EAD, OPT, L-2S are portable.
7
What tax term can you work under — W2, C2C, or 1099?A business/compliance question, but certain statuses restrict the answer — most OPT and CPT candidates cannot go 1099.

Status-Specific Validation

H4-EAD
Confirm the spouse's H-1B has an approved I-140 or AC21 extension. Request the physical EAD — code should read C(26). Confirm expiration and, if renewal is pending, the filing date relative to Oct 30, 2025.
L2-EAD / L-2S
Confirm the candidate is the spouse, not child. Ask if the I-94 shows "L-2S" — this alone is valid proof. If entry predates Jan 30, 2022, ask about a supplemental notice or physical EAD.
OPT
Request the physical EAD — confirm code C03B or C03C. Ask how many months of OPT already used. Confirm employer will complete E-Verify if STEM extension applies.
GC-EAD
Confirm I-485 is filed and pending — ask for the filing date. Confirm EAD category code reads C09. Clarify with your account manager whether the requirement accepts GC-EAD vs. a full Green Card.

Red Flags — When to Pause and Escalate

⚑Candidate cannot name their own visa status precisely — vague answers like "I'm legal to work" warrant a follow-up before proceeding.
⚑EAD expiration within 30–60 days with no renewal filed — flag explicitly to your account manager rather than assuming it will resolve.
⚑Candidate claims automatic work authorization but is on H-4, not L-2 — the single most common status confusion.
⚑CPT candidate wants to work with an employer not on their school's approved list — this can jeopardize F-1 status entirely.
⚑Any inconsistency between what the candidate says and what the document shows — verify against the physical card or I-94, not the verbal description.
The One Habit That Prevents Most Errors

Always Ask to See the Document

Rely on the actual EAD card or I-94 rather than how a candidate describes their own status. Visa terminology is used loosely in everyday conversation; the card and the I-94 are the only sources of truth.

Recruiter TipBuild these seven questions into your standard intake script rather than treating them as a special case for "complicated" candidates — asking them every time is what makes them fast and natural instead of awkward.
